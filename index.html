Research

Designing a modern computer chip is one of the most complex engineering tasks humans undertake. A cutting-edge processor has tens of billions of transistors, and getting everything right — from how the chip is coded to how it's laid out on silicon — takes years and costs hundreds of millions of dollars. Even a small mistake can mean scrapping an entire design.

My research builds AI tools that automate and accelerate key parts of this process. Think of it as giving chip engineers a very capable AI collaborator: one that can write hardware code on command, flag security vulnerabilities hiding deep in a design, and look at a chip's layout image to predict where problems will arise — before a single prototype is ever built.

LLMs for Hardware Design
Teaching AI to write and verify chip code
Hardware is described in specialized languages like Verilog — and writing correct, efficient Verilog is hard even for experts. I've built frameworks that use large language models to generate Verilog automatically, with feedback loops that optimize for power consumption, performance, and chip area simultaneously. One system — HiveGen — breaks large chip designs into a hierarchy and generates each level independently, much like how a team of engineers would divide the work. Another, LLM-VeriPPA, achieved 99.56% syntactic correctness on standard benchmarks, outperforming prior state-of-the-art.
Hardware Security
Finding malicious backdoors hidden in chip designs
Hardware Trojans are deliberately hidden flaws inserted into a chip design — they're almost invisible to standard testing, but can be triggered to cause catastrophic failures or leak sensitive data. I built Trojan-Guard, a graph neural network that treats a chip's circuit netlist as a graph and learns to spot suspicious patterns at a structural level, achieving 98.66% precision. I've also explored using LLMs with domain-specific agents and retrieval-augmented generation to enhance hardware security analysis pipelines.
Graph Learning for EDA
Accelerating chip verification with graph AI and GPU kernels
Circuit designs are naturally graphs — millions of gates connected by wires. Graph neural networks are a natural fit for reasoning over them, but standard GNN training on circuit graphs is slow and memory-hungry at scale. GROOT combines a new GNN algorithm with custom GPU kernel redesigns to speed up the verification step of logic synthesis by up to 5.8×. DR-CircuitGNN takes a similar co-design approach to accelerate GNN training itself, achieving a 4.75× speedup on large circuit datasets.
Vision Models for Physical Design
Predicting chip layout problems using computer vision
After logic is designed, it must be translated into a physical layout — specifying where every transistor sits on silicon. Layout problems like congestion and power-delivery failures are expensive to discover late. I've built generative and diffusion-based vision models that look at layout images directly and predict these issues early. One system predicts IR-drop (voltage drop across a chip's power network) by fusing image data with circuit graph information. Another predicts routing congestion with high spatial accuracy. Both are currently under review at top-tier venues — see full details.
